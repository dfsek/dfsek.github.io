The Importance of Consumers' Right to Repair

The Right to Repair is a fundamental right of consumers. Inherent in ownership of something is the ability to take it apart, and to fix it. Why are we letting corporations like Apple take that away?

Ownership

Right to Repair is, at its core, a movement about ownership. When you buy something, do you really own it? Most consumers would say yes, but upon closer inspection, many of our electronic devices are only partially owned by us. Any smart home gadget requires connections to servers run by the company that made it to function; if the company blocks your smart home device from connecting, or takes down their servers, the device is reduced to a paperweight.

Many manufacturers will block devices from accessing their servers when modifications are detected. Tesla, the famous electric car company, is notorious for blocking modified Teslas from using their Supercharger charging stations. When the manufacturer controls how you use your hardware, and whether you can disassemble, upgrade, or repair it, do you really own it?

Maintenance

Another way companies try to seize ownership of purchased hardware is by refusing to sell parts to repair it. Apple is known to deny parts to third-party repair technicians, instead requiring people to bring their device to an Apple store, where they are free to ridiculously overcharge for simple repairs.

Apple also ensures, through contracts with manufacturers, that parts can never get into the hands of consumers. If you are unable to source the parts needed to repair something you bought, do you really own the device, or are you merely "renting" it from the manufacturer, until it needs to be serviced?

"If Louis had his way, everything would be made of big, clunky plastic!"

Many misinterpret the goals of right to repair, believing that "if right to repair advocates had their way, everything would be made of big, clunky plastic with massive Phillips-head screws." Right to repair is not about making things easy to repair, it is about making them able to be repaired. Repair technicians will gladly use exotic or small screws, get through glued parts, and use pry tools to open devices, so long as they are allowed to by the manufacturer, and are provided access to the tools and parts needed to do so.

Right to repair requires no change to the design of devices, it simply requires changes to the policies manufacturers must abide by.

Supporting Right to Repair

The best way to support right to repair is by supporting grassroots movements lobbying for it, such as Louis Rossmann's Repair Preservation Group Action Fund. The Repair Preservation Group Action Fund creates petitions to show politicians that citizens support right to repair. Currently, they aim to raise $6,000,000 to get a direct-ballot initiative sent to voters to get Right to Repair legislation passed. Most voters support right to repair, since it directly benefits consumers, however getting right to repair legislation is difficult due to lobbying against it from corporations. Right to repair is a very important issue, since enforcement of it gives consumers more ownership of their devices. Getting right to repair legislation passed would benefit all consumers, since it would make devices usable longer and give the user more control.
